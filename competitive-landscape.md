# Competitive Landscape

What other game-based or digital earth-science programs do middle-school teachers already use? This file is positioning support — it helps frame MHS in outreach so teachers immediately understand where it fits among tools they already know.

The frame for outreach: **"if you like X, you'll find MHS interesting because..."** — meets teachers where they are rather than asking them to invent a new mental category.

> **Status note:** Each entry below should be verified for current state — pricing, ownership, and product lines change frequently in edtech. See [info-gathering-plan.md](info-gathering-plan.md) Priority 7.

---

## How MHS Differentiates

Before listing specific products, the cross-cutting differentiators of Mission HydroSci:

1. **DoE-funded research project.** Not a commercial product. Teachers participating are part of research — different value proposition from "buy this curriculum."
2. **Game-based with deep inquiry.** Not gamified worksheets; a genuine game-based learning experience with player-driven scientific reasoning.
3. **AI-augmented feedback (in development).** Goes beyond correctness to assess how students are thinking — a research differentiator most products don't claim.
4. **Earth science specifically (HydroSci focus on watersheds/hydrology).** Not a general science platform; deep on the earth-science slice.
5. **Free to participating teachers.** Stipend + free curriculum + free training.
6. **University-issued recognition** for participation — see [value-proposition.md](value-proposition.md).

## Commercial / Established Tools Teachers Already Use

### CK-12 Earth Science
- **What it is:** free digital textbooks, simulations, and practice systems; one of the most-used digital earth-science resources in U.S. middle schools
- **Strength:** breadth, free access, broad NGSS coverage
- **Weakness:** primarily content delivery; not deeply game-based or inquiry-driven
- **MHS positioning:** "If you use CK-12 for content delivery, MHS adds the game-based inquiry experience that CK-12 doesn't try to provide. They're complementary."

### Mystery Science (Discovery Education)
- **What it is:** lesson-based science platform; primarily K–5 originally; expanded to middle school; now part of Discovery Education
- **Strength:** highly produced video lessons, teacher-friendly, easy to deploy
- **Weakness:** more passive than active; less student-agency than game-based learning
- **MHS positioning:** "Mystery Science gives teachers great prepared lessons; MHS gives students an inquiry-driven gameplay experience that complements direct instruction."

### BrainPOP Earth Science
- **What it is:** animated educational videos with quizzes, popular K–8
- **Strength:** student appeal, broad subject coverage
- **Weakness:** primarily content consumption; limited inquiry/agency
- **MHS positioning:** "BrainPOP keeps students engaged in passive learning; MHS asks students to actually do science."

### Generation Genius
- **What it is:** NGSS-aligned video lessons with hands-on activities, K–8
- **Strength:** clean NGSS alignment; teacher-friendly format
- **Weakness:** video-driven; not deeply interactive
- **MHS positioning:** "Generation Genius is great for video-led lessons; MHS adds an interactive game-based experience for the inquiry-cycle elements students retain best."

### Tyto Online
- **What it is:** game-based science learning platform for middle school; closer to MHS in pedagogy
- **Strength:** real game-based learning; serious science content
- **Weakness:** broader scope; less earth-science-specific than MHS
- **MHS positioning:** "Tyto Online and MHS are pedagogically aligned. MHS goes deeper on earth-science (especially water/hydrology) and is currently a research project — different value proposition."

### Filament Games
- **What it is:** edtech game studio with multiple titles, including some earth/space science
- **Strength:** high-quality games; strong educational design
- **Weakness:** scattered across multiple titles, not deeply earth-science-focused
- **MHS positioning:** "If you've used Filament titles, you understand the value of game-based learning. MHS is a deep dive on earth science specifically, with research-grade assessment integrated."

### Smithsonian Science Education Center / STEM in 30
- **What it is:** Smithsonian-produced K–8 science programming; STEM in 30 is short-form classroom-friendly modules
- **Strength:** strong content credibility; institutional brand
- **Weakness:** content delivery rather than student-driven inquiry
- **MHS positioning:** "Smithsonian materials provide strong content; MHS adds the inquiry-driven student experience that complements high-quality content."

### Stile Education
- **What it is:** Australian-origin middle-school science platform; growing U.S. footprint; NGSS-aligned
- **Strength:** modern UI, strong NGSS alignment, teacher-friendly tooling
- **Weakness:** comprehensive curriculum platform, requires school adoption commitment
- **MHS positioning:** "Stile is a curriculum replacement; MHS is a research-grade learning experience that drops into your existing curriculum without replacement."

### Legends of Learning
- **What it is:** game-based science learning platform with hundreds of short games per topic
- **Strength:** breadth of games; teacher-friendly assignment system
- **Weakness:** breadth over depth; no single game gives the deep inquiry experience MHS targets
- **MHS positioning:** "Legends of Learning has many short games; MHS is a deep multi-session experience, more like a unit than a quick activity."

## Open / Public Resources Teachers Use

### USGS, NASA, NOAA Education Materials
- **What they are:** free federal earth-science K–12 resources
- **Use case:** primary data, classroom activities, real-world examples
- **MHS positioning:** "USGS / NASA materials give students real data; MHS gives them an immersive context to apply scientific reasoning to problems."

### NGSS Hub (BSCS / NextGenScience)
- **What it is:** curated NGSS resources
- **Use case:** alignment reference for teachers planning units
- **MHS positioning:** "MHS aligns to NGSS PEs in the earth-science strand; the curriculum we provide includes NGSS crosswalks teachers can hand to administrators."

## Game-Based Learning Adjacents (Not Earth-Science-Specific)

These are not direct competitors but shape what teachers expect from "game-based learning":

- **Minecraft Education** — block-building learning; broad, generic
- **Kerbal Space Program (in classrooms)** — teacher-driven; strong physics; less earth-science
- **PhET Simulations** — university-built simulations, including some earth-science; widely used
- **Gizmos / ExploreLearning** — interactive simulations; extensive earth-science library

These shape expectations. If a teacher says "we already use PhET / Gizmos," the right framing is: "Those are great single-concept simulations; MHS is a game-based experience that puts those concepts in an inquiry context where students have to make decisions and see consequences."

## Outreach Application

When responding to a teacher who mentions an existing tool, use the table above to frame MHS:

> "We use Mystery Science for our earth-science unit."
> "Mystery Science gives you great prepared lessons. MHS sits alongside that — it's an inquiry-driven gameplay experience your students would do *as* the lab/inquiry portion. We're a research project, so participating teachers get a stipend, free training, and the curriculum is designed to integrate with whatever you're already using."

This framing converts mentions of competitors into bridges to MHS rather than walls.

## Risk: Crowded Field

The earth-science middle-school edtech space is dense, and many teachers are tool-fatigued. The pitch must be:

- **Different in kind, not just degree** — MHS is research, not a product. That's the differentiator.
- **Free + paid (stipend) for the teacher** — overwhelms most "yet another tool" objections.
- **Time-bounded commitment** — a single research deployment, not a permanent platform adoption.

Lead with these three points when teachers compare MHS to existing tools.
